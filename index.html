Proposal for a Provincial Virtual Nursing Program in Ontario

A formal, research-backed blueprint for enhancing patient care and nursing workforce sustainability through a government-funded virtual nursing model.

Introduction and Background

Ontario faces a severe nursing workforce challenge, with a projected shortfall of 33,000 nurses by 2028. This shortage demands innovative models of care delivery to maximize the reach and impact of our experienced Registered Nurses (RNs). One such innovation is virtual nursing; this leverages technology to allow RNs to provide patient care remotely. Virtual nursing gained traction during the pandemic and is now being systematically scaled by many health systems as a strategy to support bedside teams and retain veteran nurses.

This report proposes a provincial, government-funded virtual nursing program in Ontario that enables experienced RNs to work from home as “virtual nurses.” Unlike ad-hoc hospital or agency-run initiatives, a centrally administered program would ensure equitable access to virtual nursing services across the province. It would standardize quality and integrate strong guardrails to complement bedside care. The goal is a formal blueprint for the Ontario Ministry of Health’s Nursing and Professional Practice Division to consider.

Government-Employer Model

A centralized approach promotes consistency, prevents competition between hospitals, and leverages economies of scale.

Rationale for a Provincial Model

A provincially funded and managed virtual RN program provides several advantages over individual hospital or private agency approaches. Centralized oversight and funding would promote consistency in standards, training, and technology. This ensures that smaller or rural hospitals can benefit equally from virtual nursing support. Ontario has precedents for successful province-wide telehealth services. For example, the Health811 nurse advice line handles about 70,000 calls/chats per month from across Ontario via a centralized platform.

From a workforce perspective, a government-employer model permits better coordination of virtual RN deployment to areas of greatest need. It prevents a patchwork of differing hospital programs that compete for staff or develop redundant infrastructure. A provincial program can also set uniform employment conditions and collaborate with the Ontario Nurses’ Association (ONA). This ensures these remote roles support frontline staffing rather than undermine it.

Virtual RN Use Cases and Benefits

Discharge Education & Coordination

Virtual RNs can take charge of discharge planning and patient teaching. At Baptist Memorial Hospital, virtual nurses conduct discharge education for surgical patients, fill out paperwork, and arrange to print discharge instructions. This process typically takes 15 minutes and ensures patients receive thorough counseling on medications and follow-up care. Dedicated discharge teaching has reduced readmissions in multiple jurisdictions.

Admission Interview & Documentation

Admission assessments involve history-taking and documentation rather than hands-on care. Virtual RNs can guide patients through admission questions and enter data directly into the electronic health record (EHR). Henry Ford Health identified admissions as a primary task to shift to virtual care. This accelerates bed flow and maintains documentation accuracy.

Patient Education & Consults

Virtual RNs provide ongoing patient education during the hospital stay. They reinforce pre-surgery instructions and answer routine questions. Virtual nurses act as another approachable face of care, which increases patient satisfaction. They can also coordinate consults by arranging virtual family meetings or specialist consultations.

Safety Monitoring ("Virtual Sitter")

Hospitals often require 1:1 patient sitters for those at risk of falls or delirium. Virtual "tele-sitter" technology allows one staff member to remotely monitor multiple patients via cameras. Virtual RNs can oversee camera feeds for acute cases where an RN’s assessment skills might detect subtle signs of distress. This adds a layer of fall prevention without pulling bedside staff away.

Low-Contention Rollout Strategy with Guardrails

Introducing virtual nursing into Ontario’s healthcare system requires careful planning to gain buy-in from frontline staff and unions. We propose a phased rollout starting with a pilot project under strict guardrails.

No Reduction in Bedside RN Positions

Participating hospitals must commit that virtual RNs will not result in any reduction of budgeted in-person RN staffing lines. The virtual program augments capacity; it does not replace it.

Clear Delineation of Tasks

The program must establish a defined scope of practice. Virtual RNs may document admission history but will not perform wound care or administer medications.

Mandated Reporting

Hospitals must transparently track virtual RN usage. Data on tasks performed and key outcomes will allow evaluation of the program’s impact.

Capped Pilot Scope

The initial rollout should be limited in size and duration. We recommend a 12-month pilot with roughly 50 RNs across volunteer hospitals.

Examples in Practice

Henry Ford Health (Michigan, USA)

Henry Ford launched a virtual acute care nursing program across its hospital system in 2025. Virtual RNs operate from a 24/7 command center and appear on patients’ in-room smart TVs via secure video. They perform admission interviews, discharge prep, safety rounds, and chart checks. In the first full month, virtual nurses logged 600+ hours on patient calls and completed approximately 24,700 tasks.

Baptist Memorial Health Care (Southern USA)

Baptist began implementing virtual nurses in 2022. They utilize telepresence robots that the remote nurse can drive to patient rooms. Virtual nurses focus on admissions and discharges. By 2024, Baptist Memphis had virtual nurses handle approximately 6,000 hours of admission and discharge assessments in a year.

Canadian Telehealth Nursing Services

Ontario’s Health811 nurse hotline and Telehomecare program demonstrate that nurses can successfully deliver remote assessments and health education at scale. Ottawa’s Telehome Monitoring program achieved major improvements in outcomes and cost savings, illustrating the potential impact of remote nursing care.

Technology Infrastructure and Tools

  • Secure Remote Access to EHRs: Virtual RNs require secure VPN or cloud-based access to systems like Meditech, Cerner, or Epic to view charts and enter documentation in real time.
  • Video and Audio Communication: Implement standardized platforms for two-way video, such as wall-mounted screens or mobile carts. Systems must be HIPAA/PHIPA-compliant.
  • AI-Powered Documentation: Incorporate ambient clinical intelligence tools like Nuance Dragon Ambient eXperience (DAX) to assist with charting. This allows conversation to be auto-transcribed and summarized.
  • Home Office Hardware: The government should cover costs for high-performance computers, HD webcams, and secure network connectivity for all virtual RNs.

Operational Considerations

Scheduling and Shifts

We recommend starting with 8-hour shifts to maintain focus. Coverage will likely be needed 18–24 hours a day. Hospitals will book virtual RN time slots for predictable tasks and have the ability to call on them for urgent needs.

Workflow Integration

Each hospital unit needs protocols for engaging the virtual nurse. A regional hub or software platform will triage requests. Regular virtual huddles between on-duty virtual RNs and site liaisons will ensure smooth operations.

HR and Unions

Virtual RNs could be employed by Ontario Health or seconded from hospitals. Alignment with ONA contracts is necessary to ensure parity in wages and benefits.

Quality Assurance

Continuous evaluation is required. Key performance indicators include patient satisfaction, nurse burnout levels, documentation accuracy, and clinical outcomes like readmission rates.

Conclusion and Recommendations

Ontario’s Nursing and Professional Practice Division has an opportunity to lead a transformative initiative. This proposal lays out the justification for a government-employer model that enables experienced RNs to provide virtual care. This is additive; it extends the reach of every nurse and relieves burden from on-site staff.

Key Recommendations

  1. Launch a Pilot in 2026: Convene a project team early in the year and select 3–5 diverse hospitals.
  2. Establish Governance: Create a steering committee and partner with technology providers.
  3. Develop Training: Conduct simulations and training for both virtual and unit staff.
  4. Monitor Rigorously: Define KPIs and produce a comprehensive evaluation after 12 months.
  5. Plan for Scale-Up: Draft an expansion strategy while maintaining core guardrails.

Workforce Strategy

Ontario Nursing Pool and Scheduling Implementation

Introduction and Background on addressing staffing shortages through centralized solutions.

The Agency Challenge

Ontario lacks a health workforce strategy with provincial and regional solutions to address nursing staffing shortages. Ontario’s public healthcare system has also seen a surging reliance on private nursing agencies, which has strained hospital and long-term care (LTC) budgets. Between 2013 and 2023, real per-capita spending on private agency staff in Ontario nearly doubled (a 98% increase) even as direct hospital spending grew only 6%.

Ontario hospitals paid over $9.2 billion to private, for-profit staffing agencies in the last decade, despite agency nurses accounting for only a tiny fraction (0.4%) of total hours worked. In 2022–2023 alone, hospitals and LTC homes spent almost $1 billion on agency nurses and personal support workers to cover assignments. This stopgap comes at a high price: agencies often charge double or even triple the normal hourly rate of in-house staff. For example, some Ontario LTC facilities have paid $88–$150/hour for agency RNs (versus about $43/hour in wages for a staff nurse) and even higher short-notice premiums. One small hospital reported rates up to $300/hour for agency nurses.

These practices have created a difficult cycle – higher pay and flexibility in agency work lure nurses from the public system, worsening staff shortages and forcing hospitals to rely even more on agencies. Now is the time for policymakers and stakeholders to explore a provincial nursing pool as a solution to reduce costs and stabilize the workforce by providing a centralized, public alternative to private agencies.

Financial Impact

Agency expenditures have skyrocketed since the COVID-19 pandemic. In 2022–23, total agency staffing costs neared $953 million.

Financial Impact of Private Agency Nursing

Recent reports highlight the growing financial burden of agency nursing on Ontario’s healthcare budget. A 2025 analysis by the Canadian Centre for Policy Alternatives (“Hollowed Out” report) revealed Ontario’s public hospitals paid $9.2 billion to private agencies over 10 years. Notably, agency-supplied staff accounted for only 0.4% of total worked hours but consumed about 6% of total nurse staffing costs, due to their premium rates.

Agency usage roughly doubled year-over-year: in hospitals, agency nurses provided 1.5% of hours in 2022–23 (up from 0.7% the year prior). This trend prompted warnings that “agency costs are crowding out the budgets for care” in core services. The current model is cost-inefficient, as private agencies charge 2–3 times the cost of hiring regular staff. Any plan for an Ontario nursing pool must address this financial imperative: rein in exorbitant agency fees and recapture budget capacity for public healthcare services.

Models of Centralized Nursing Staffing Pools

Other jurisdictions have created centralized or government-sponsored nursing pools to reduce agency dependence. Common success factors include maintaining more than union-level pay/benefits for pool nurses, centralized scheduling systems, and support for travel or training.

Jurisdiction Program Key Features
United Kingdom NHS Professionals National “bank” of temporary staff. 39M hours filled in 2021-22. Central recruitment and booking; self-funded via fees charged to hospitals.
British Columbia GoHealth BC Float pool of RNs who are regular health system employees. Nurses receive union wages, benefits, pension. Travel support provided.
Manitoba Provincial Travel Nurse Team Internal float pool for flexible deployments. Created via union agreement to offer an alternative to agency gigs.
Quebec Regional Float Pools Phasing out private agencies entirely by 2025. Expanded internal float pools ensure coverage. Law imposes fines for non-compliance.

Labor Relations and Legal Feasibility

Any Ontario nursing pool must navigate the labor relations context, particularly the Ontario Nurses’ Association (ONA) collective agreements. The central ONA–Hospital agreement explicitly states the hospital “shall not contract out” bargaining unit nursing duties if it results in layoffs or loss of hours for union nurses. It carves out only a narrow exception for truly ad hoc, single-shift agency usage to cover unexpected absences.

The most feasible approach is to ensure the provincial pool operates within the public sector. For example, pool nurses could be employees of a new crown agency or of Ontario Health, with compensation aligned to ONA collective agreements.

If pool nurses are not hospital employees or not covered by the ONA contract, the union may argue this violates no-contracting-out provisions. The safest path is treating the pool as an extension of hospital scheduling rather than a separate employer, which preserves union protection and removes contractor-misclassification risks.

Infrastructure Needs: Technology and Logistics

Centralized Scheduling

A central scheduling platform is critical for matching pool nurses to vacant shifts. This should function as a real-time marketplace where hospitals post staffing needs and nurses can sign up for available shifts via mobile app.

Credentialing Management

A centralized database should store and verify licenses, specialty certifications, and immunization status. Automating license verification speeds up onboarding new pool members.

Information Systems Compatibility

Pool nurses must navigate different EMR systems (Epic, Cerner, Meditech). The program needs to invest in standardized EMR training so nurses can quickly learn the essentials of different systems before an assignment.

Logistics and Support

Coordination of travel and housing is needed when nurses deploy outside their home area. A 24/7 support line ensures backup if a nurse has an issue finding a unit or if a shift changes last-minute.

Implementation Roadmap

1. Feasibility & Consultation (Months 0–3)

Establish project team & advisory table (MOH, Ontario Health, ONA, OHA). Analyze agency spend. Address legal/HR feasibility. Secure stakeholder buy-in on the public-sector pool vision.

2. Governance & Framework (Months 3–6)

Decide host organization and secure funding. Draft enabling policy (e.g. require hospitals to use pool first). Design operational policies like travel reimbursement and nurse employment terms.

3. Recruitment & Onboarding (Months 4–9)

Launch recruitment targeting agency nurses. Process applications through centralized verification. Conduct training/orientation for first cohorts (EMR systems, clinical protocols).

4. Pilot Deployment (Months 9–15)

Go-live in pilot hospitals/LTC homes. Pool nurses begin filling assignments. Provide 24/7 support. Collect data on fill rates and costs. Produce evaluation report.

5. Scale-Up Rollout (Months 15–24)

Incorporate pilot learnings. Sequentially onboard remaining regions. Enforce policy shifts: hospitals now required to utilize pool for temps; begin phasing out existing agency contracts.

6. Full Implementation (Month 24+)

Pool fully operational province-wide. Comprehensive outcome evaluation. Formalize long-term governance. Consider legislative ban on agencies beyond this point if pool is meeting needs.

Conclusion

By following these implementation steps, Ontario can create a centralized nursing pool that addresses the root causes behind the agency boom. This strategy shifts the solution to the public realm – offering nurses flexible work opportunities with the security and standards of the public system. Financially, redirecting funds from agencies to an in-house pool should yield significant savings and better value for money, while also improving workforce morale and retention.

A well-implemented Ontario Nursing Pool offers a feasible path to safeguarding the sustainability of the public healthcare budget.
